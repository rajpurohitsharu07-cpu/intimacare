Myth vs Fact

Clear scientific explanations of common sexual health misconceptions. Educational only, 18+.

Myth: Erectile dysfunction only affects older men.

Fact: ED can affect men of all ages due to stress, lifestyle, or medical conditions.

Myth: Premature ejaculation is always caused by anxiety.

Fact: While anxiety contributes, physiological factors, hormonal imbalance, or lifestyle can also play a role.

Myth: Low libido is only psychological.

Fact: Low libido can result from hormonal changes, medications, chronic illness, or psychological factors.

Myth: Performance anxiety is rare and only affects men.

Fact: Performance anxiety is common in both men and women and often linked to stress or expectations.

Myth: Testosterone is the only hormone that affects sexual health.

Fact: Estrogen, progesterone, oxytocin, and prolactin also influence libido, mood, and sexual function.

Myth: Women cannot experience sexual desire during menstruation.

Fact: Libido may vary; many women experience sexual desire at different points of the cycle.

Myth: All women orgasm the same way as men.

Fact: Female orgasm can be achieved through multiple stimulation types, with variation among individuals.

Myth: Masturbation reduces fertility or causes impotence.

Fact: Masturbation is normal and has no adverse long-term effects on fertility or sexual function.

Myth: Using condoms reduces pleasure drastically.

Fact: Modern condoms are designed for comfort; pleasure can be maintained with correct use.

Myth: Fertility ends immediately after age 30.

Fact: Fertility declines gradually; many can conceive naturally in their 30s with healthy lifestyle.

Myth: Watching pornography causes permanent sexual dysfunction.

Fact: Excessive consumption can affect perception, but moderate, mindful use generally has no long-term harm.

Myth: STIs only affect promiscuous people.

Fact: Anyone sexually active is at risk. Protection and regular check-ups are essential.

Myth: Sexual orientation can be changed through therapy.

Fact: Orientation is innate; conversion attempts are ineffective and harmful.

Myth: Older adults cannot have an active sex life.

Fact: Sexual activity can continue at any age; frequency and libido vary individually.

Myth: High testosterone always improves sexual performance.

Fact: Balance is key; excessive levels can cause negative effects like aggression or hormonal imbalance.

Myth: Sex during pregnancy is always unsafe.

Fact: In healthy pregnancies, sexual activity is generally safe unless advised otherwise by a doctor.

Myth: Pain during intercourse is normal and unavoidable.

Fact: Persistent pain is abnormal and may indicate underlying medical conditions; consultation recommended.
