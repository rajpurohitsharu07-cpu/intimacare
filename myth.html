Myth vs Fact

Clear scientific explanations of common sexual health misconceptions. Educational only, 18+.

Myth: Erectile dysfunction only affects older men.

Fact: ED can affect men of all ages. While age can increase risk due to hormonal decline, younger men may also experience ED due to stress, anxiety, lifestyle factors like smoking, alcohol, poor diet, or lack of exercise.

Example: A healthy 28-year-old may face ED in high-pressure situations. Awareness and early lifestyle modifications or professional consultation can improve outcomes.

Tip: Exercise regularly, maintain balanced nutrition, manage stress, sleep well, and avoid excess alcohol or smoking.

Myth: Premature ejaculation is always caused by anxiety.

Fact: PE may result from psychological stress, but also from hormonal imbalances, medical conditions, or lifestyle factors.

Example: Young men with high stress may ejaculate quickly despite healthy physiology. Combination of therapy, lifestyle adjustment, and medical guidance helps.

Tip: Relaxation, communication with partner, and avoiding excessive alcohol improve control.

Myth: Low libido is always psychological.

Fact: Libido can be affected by hormones (testosterone, estrogen), chronic illness, medications, stress, or relationship factors.

Example: Both men and women may notice reduced sexual desire due to sleep deprivation or thyroid issues.

Tip: Healthy sleep, exercise, stress management, and balanced nutrition enhance sexual desire.

Myth: Performance anxiety is rare and only affects men.

Fact: It is common in both genders and can impact sexual satisfaction. Causes include stress, self-expectation, and relationship pressure.

Tip: Mindfulness, open communication, and focusing on intimacy rather than performance reduce anxiety.

Myth: Only testosterone affects sexual health.

Fact: Estrogen, progesterone, oxytocin, and prolactin also play key roles in libido, mood, and sexual function.

Tip: Balanced diet, sleep, and exercise help maintain healthy hormonal levels.

Myth: Women cannot experience sexual desire during menstruation.

Fact: Desire varies; some women report increased arousal during menstruation due to hormonal changes.

Tip: Comfort, communication, and hygiene help maintain safe sexual activity during periods.

Myth: Female orgasm occurs the same way as male orgasm.

Fact: Female orgasm can be achieved through multiple types of stimulation. Response varies widely among individuals.

Tip: Communication with partner and exploration improve sexual satisfaction.

Myth: Masturbation causes infertility or impotence.

Fact: Masturbation is normal and has no long-term negative effect on fertility or sexual function.

Tip: Healthy sexual expression includes masturbation without guilt or harm.

Myth: Condoms reduce pleasure drastically.

Fact: Modern condoms are designed for comfort and protection; pleasure can be maintained with proper use.

Myth: Fertility ends abruptly at 30.

Fact: Fertility declines gradually; many can conceive naturally in their 30s with healthy lifestyle habits.

Myth: Watching pornography causes permanent sexual dysfunction.

Fact: Excessive consumption may affect perception, but moderate, mindful use does not cause long-term harm.

Myth: STIs only affect promiscuous people.

Fact: Anyone sexually active is at risk. Protection and regular check-ups are important.

Myth: Natural lubrication is always sufficient.

Fact: Lubrication varies due to hormones, stress, or medical issues. Additional lubrication may be necessary.

Myth: Sexual orientation can be changed.

Fact: Orientation is innate; attempts to change it are ineffective and harmful.

Myth: Older adults cannot have an active sex life.

Fact: Sexual activity can continue at any age; libido varies individually.

Myth: High testosterone always improves sexual performance.

Fact: Balance is key; excessive levels may cause negative effects such as aggression or hormonal imbalance.

Myth: Sex during pregnancy is always unsafe.

Fact: In healthy pregnancies, sexual activity is generally safe unless otherwise advised by a doctor.

Myth: Pain during intercourse is normal and unavoidable.

Fact: Persistent pain may indicate underlying medical conditions. Consultation is recommended.
